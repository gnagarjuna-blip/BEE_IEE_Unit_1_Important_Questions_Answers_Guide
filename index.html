Kirchhoff's Laws (KCL & KVL)

1. Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL)

Statement (2M): The algebraic sum of currents meeting at a junction (node) in an electrical circuit is zero.

Detailed Explanation: KCL is based on the Law of Conservation of Charge. Since charge cannot accumulate at a node, the total current entering must equal the total current leaving.

$$\sum I_{incoming} = \sum I_{outgoing}$$
Figure 1.1: KCL at a Node. Equation: I1 + I2 = I3 + I4

2. Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL)

Statement (2M): The algebraic sum of all voltages (EMFs and IR drops) in any closed loop of a network is zero.

Detailed Explanation: KVL is based on the Law of Conservation of Energy. It states that the energy supplied by the sources in a loop is exactly consumed by the components in that same loop.

$$\sum V_{sources} = \sum (I \times R)$$
Figure 1.2: KVL Closed Loop. Equation: Vs - I(R1) - I(R2) = 0

Active and Passive Elements

Circuit elements are categorized by how they interact with electrical energy over time.

Feature Active Elements Passive Elements
Definition Elements that supply/generate net energy to the circuit. Elements that store or dissipate energy.
Function Energy Donors. Can provide power gain (amplification). Energy Acceptors. Cannot provide power gain.
Examples Voltage Sources, Current Sources, Transistors, Batteries. Resistors, Inductors, Capacitors.
Key Concept: While Inductors (L) and Capacitors (C) temporarily store energy, they are still considered Passive because they cannot generate energy internally; they can only return energy they previously received from an active source. Resistors (R) dissipate energy as heat.

Voltage vs. Current Sources

A. Voltage Source

  • Ideal: Provides a constant terminal voltage regardless of the current drawn. Internal resistance is zero ($R_{int}=0$).
  • Practical: Due to material limitations, it has a small internal resistance connected in series ($R_{se}$). As load current ($I_L$) increases, the terminal voltage ($V_t$) drops due to the internal drop ($I_L \times R_{se}$).

B. Current Source

  • Ideal: Provides a constant current regardless of the terminal voltage. Internal resistance is infinite ($R_{int}=\infty$).
  • Practical: Has a very high internal resistance connected in parallel ($R_{sh}$). As terminal voltage increases, some current leaks through the internal resistor, slightly reducing the output current.

Series R-C Circuit Analysis

Given Data: Resistance $R = 10 \Omega$, Capacitance $C = 0.1 F$, Constant Voltage $V = 20 V$ applied at $t = 0$.

Step 1: Determine Time Constant ($\tau$)

The time constant determines the speed of charging/discharging.

$$\tau = R \times C = 10 \Omega \times 0.1 F = 1 \text{ second}$$

Step 2: Obtain Current Equation $i(t)$

At the instant the switch closes ($t=0$), the uncharged capacitor acts as a short circuit. The initial current is maximum:

$I_0 = \frac{V}{R} = \frac{20}{10} = 2A$.

The current decays exponentially as the capacitor charges:

$$i(t) = I_0 e^{-t/\tau} = 2 e^{-t} \text{ Amperes}$$

Step 3: Determine Voltages

  • Voltage across Resistor ($V_R$): By Ohm's law, $V_R = i(t) \times R$.
    $$V_R(t) = (2 e^{-t}) \times 10 = 20 e^{-t} \text{ Volts}$$
  • Voltage across Capacitor ($V_C$): By KVL, $V = V_R + V_C$, so $V_C = V - V_R$.
    $$V_C(t) = 20 - 20 e^{-t} = 20(1 - e^{-t}) \text{ Volts}$$

V-I Characteristics of Passive Elements (R, L, C)

This explains how Voltage (V) and Current (I) relate in phase for different components when subjected to AC.

1. Resistor (R)

In a pure resistor, voltage and current are in phase. They reach their maximum and zero values simultaneously.

$$V = IR$$

2. Inductor (L)

In a pure inductor, the Voltage leads the Current by 90° ($\pi/2$ rad). The inductor opposes changes in current. Energy is stored in the magnetic field.

$$v(t) = L \frac{di}{dt}$$

3. Capacitor (C)

In a pure capacitor, the Current leads the Voltage by 90° ($\pi/2$ rad). The capacitor opposes changes in voltage. Energy is stored in the electric field.

$$i(t) = C \frac{dv}{dt}$$

Superposition Theorem

Statement: In any linear, bilateral network containing two or more independent sources, the response (current or voltage) in any element is the algebraic sum of the responses produced by each source acting alone, while all other sources are replaced by their internal resistances.

Steps for Analysis (10M Procedure):

  1. Consider one independent source at a time.
  2. Deactivate all other independent sources:
    • Replace Voltage Sources with a Short Circuit (0V).
    • Replace Current Sources with an Open Circuit (0A).
  3. Calculate the desired current/voltage produced by the single active source using standard circuit analysis (KCL/KVL/Ohm's law).
  4. Repeat the process for every independent source in the circuit.
  5. The total response is the algebraic sum of individual responses. (Note direction/polarity).

Constraint: Superposition is valid only for linear circuits. It cannot be used directly to calculate power, as power ($P=I^2R$) is a non-linear relationship.

Thevenin's and Norton's Theorems

These theorems allow complex linear networks to be simplified into a single source and a single resistor with respect to two load terminals (A and B).

1. Thevenin’s Theorem

Statement (2M): Any two-terminal linear DC network can be replaced by an equivalent circuit consisting of a single voltage source ($V_{th}$) in series with a single resistance ($R_{th}$).

Figure 7.1: Thevenin Equivalent Circuit

2. Norton’s Theorem

Statement (2M): Any two-terminal linear DC network can be replaced by an equivalent circuit consisting of a single current source ($I_n$) in parallel with a single resistance ($R_n$).

Figure 7.2: Norton Equivalent Circuit

Key Relationships (For 10M Explanations):

  • $V_{th}$ (Thevenin Voltage): The Open Circuit Voltage ($V_{oc}$) measured across terminals A-B.
  • $I_n$ (Norton Current): The Short Circuit Current ($I_{sc}$) measured flowing between terminals A-B when shorted.
  • $R_{th} = R_n$: The equivalent resistance of the network looking into terminals A-B when all independent sources are deactivated.

Source Transformation relation: $V_{th} = I_n \times R_{th}$
