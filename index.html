Unit 1: DC Circuits - Complete Theory and Analysis

1. State and Explain KCL and KVL (10 Marks)

A. Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

Statement: In any electrical network, the algebraic sum of currents meeting at a point (node) is zero. In other words, the sum of currents entering a junction is exactly equal to the sum of currents leaving that junction.

Sum of I_in = Sum of I_out
Figure 1.1: Current distribution at a Node. Equation: I1 + I2 = I3 + I4

Explanation: KCL is a statement of the Law of Conservation of Charge. Since a node has no capacity to store or generate charge, every electron that enters must leave immediately. This law is applicable to lumped parameter circuits.

B. Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

Statement: In any closed path (loop) of an electrical circuit, the algebraic sum of all EMFs plus the algebraic sum of all voltage drops (IR products) is zero.

Sum of V = 0
Figure 1.2: A closed circuit loop. Equation: V_s - (I * R1) - (I * R2) = 0

Explanation: KVL represents the Law of Conservation of Energy. It states that the work done in moving a unit charge around any closed path is zero. This means the energy provided by the battery is exactly dissipated by the resistors in the loop.

2. Explain Active and Passive Elements (5-10 Marks)

Electrical circuit elements are broadly classified into two categories based on their energy-handling capabilities.

Figure 2.1: Energy transfer from Active to Passive components.
Feature Active Elements Passive Elements
Definition Elements that generate or supply electrical energy to a circuit. Elements that receive, consume, or store electrical energy.
Power Gain Capable of providing power gain (amplification). Incapable of providing any power gain.
Nature Energy Donors. Energy Acceptors.
Examples Batteries, DC Generators, Solar Cells, Transistors. Resistors, Inductors, Capacitors.
Key Concept: While inductors and capacitors store energy, they are still considered Passive because they cannot generate energy from a non-electrical source; they can only return energy previously received from an active source.
