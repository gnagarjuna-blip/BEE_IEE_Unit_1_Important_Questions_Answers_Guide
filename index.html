Kirchhoff's Laws (KCL & KVL)

1. Kirchhoff’s Current Law (KCL)

Statement (2M): The algebraic sum of currents meeting at a junction (node) in an electrical circuit is zero.

Detailed Explanation: KCL is based on the Law of Conservation of Charge. Since charge cannot accumulate at a node, the total current entering must equal the total current leaving.

$$\sum I_{incoming} = \sum I_{outgoing}$$

2. Kirchhoff’s Voltage Law (KVL)

Statement (2M): The algebraic sum of all voltages (EMFs and IR drops) in any closed loop of a network is zero.

Detailed Explanation: KVL is based on the Law of Conservation of Energy. It states that the energy supplied by the sources in a loop is exactly consumed by the components in that same loop.

$$\sum V_{sources} = \sum (I \times R)$$

Active and Passive Elements

Circuit elements are categorized by how they interact with electrical energy over time.

Feature Active Elements Passive Elements
Definition Elements that supply/generate net energy to the circuit. Elements that store or dissipate energy.
Function Energy Donors. Can provide power gain. Energy Acceptors. Cannot provide power gain.
Examples Voltage Sources, Current Sources, Transistors, Batteries. Resistors, Inductors, Capacitors.
Note: Inductors and Capacitors are passive even though they "give back" energy, because they can only return what they previously absorbed.

Voltage vs. Current Sources

Voltage Source

Ideal: Provides a constant voltage regardless of the current drawn. Internal resistance is zero.

Practical: Has a small internal resistance in series ($R_s$), causing the terminal voltage to drop as current increases.

Current Source

Ideal: Provides a constant current regardless of the terminal voltage. Internal resistance is infinite.

Practical: Has a high internal resistance in parallel ($R_{sh}$), causing the output current to decrease as terminal voltage rises.

Series R-C Circuit Analysis

Problem: $R = 10 \Omega$, $C = 0.1 F$, $V = 20 V$ applied at $t = 0$.

1. Time Constant ($\tau$)

$$\tau = R \times C = 10 \times 0.1 = 1 \text{ second}$$

2. Current Equation $i(t)$

Initial current $I_0 = V/R = 20/10 = 2A$. The current decays exponentially:

$$i(t) = I_0 e^{-t/\tau} = 2 e^{-t} \text{ Amperes}$$

3. Voltage Determinations

  • Resistor Voltage ($V_R$): $V_R(t) = i(t) \cdot R = 20 e^{-t} \text{ Volts}$
  • Capacitor Voltage ($V_C$): $V_C(t) = V(1 - e^{-t/\tau}) = 20(1 - e^{-t}) \text{ Volts}$

V-I Characteristics of R, L, and C

1. Resistor (R)

Follows Ohm’s Law. Voltage and Current are in phase.

$$V = I \times R$$

2. Inductor (L)

Voltage leads current by $90^\circ$. It stores energy in a magnetic field.

$$v(t) = L \frac{di}{dt}$$

3. Capacitor (C)

Current leads voltage by $90^\circ$. It stores energy in an electric field.

$$i(t) = C \frac{dv}{dt}$$

Superposition Theorem

Statement: In any linear, bilateral network containing more than one source, the response in any element is the algebraic sum of the responses produced by each source acting alone.

Steps for Analysis:

  1. Select one independent source and deactivate others.
  2. Deactivation: Replace Voltage sources with Short Circuits and Current sources with Open Circuits.
  3. Calculate the current/voltage in the desired branch.
  4. Repeat for all sources and sum the results algebraically.
Limitation: Applicable only to Linear circuits. It cannot be used to calculate Power directly (since power is $I^2R$, a non-linear relation).

Thevenin's and Norton's Theorems

1. Thevenin’s Theorem

Statement (2M): Any two-terminal linear network can be replaced by an equivalent circuit consisting of a voltage source $V_{th}$ in series with a resistance $R_{th}$.

[Image of Thevenin equivalent circuit]

2. Norton’s Theorem

Statement (2M): Any two-terminal linear network can be replaced by an equivalent circuit consisting of a current source $I_n$ in parallel with a resistance $R_n$.

Comparison & Relation

Thevenin and Norton are duals of each other. They are related by Source Transformation:

$$R_{th} = R_n \quad \text{and} \quad V_{th} = I_n \times R_{th}$$
